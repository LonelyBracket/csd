5 Metrics That Actually Matter for Platform Teams

Stop tracking vanity metrics. Here's what successful platform engineering teams measure to prove value and drive adoption.

Sarah Chen

Staff Engineer at Spotify

When I started leading the platform team at Spotify, we made a classic mistake: we measured everything we could, then drowned in dashboards nobody looked at. It took us six months to realize we were tracking the wrong things entirely.

The problem with most platform metrics is they focus on what the platform does rather than what it enables. Your executives don't care how many deployments your CI/CD pipeline processed. They care whether engineering teams are shipping faster and more reliably.

1. Developer Time to First Deployment

This is the single most important metric for any platform team. How long does it take a new developer—or a developer on a new project—to go from zero to their first production deployment?

At Spotify, we obsessed over this number. When we started, it averaged 3 weeks. After two years of focused platform work, we got it down to 4 hours. That's not just a nice number—it fundamentally changed how teams approached new projects.

"If your platform takes more than a day for a competent engineer to deploy something meaningful, you have work to do."

Here's how to measure it properly:

  • Start the clock when a developer joins a team or starts a new service
  • Stop when they've deployed working code to production
  • Track the median, not the mean (outliers will skew your data)
  • Segment by team size and project type

2. Platform Adoption Rate

The best platform in the world is worthless if nobody uses it. Adoption rate tells you whether your platform is actually solving problems people have, or whether you're building features nobody asked for.

Calculate it simply: (Teams using platform / Total teams) × 100

But here's the nuance most teams miss: track voluntary adoption separately from mandated adoption. If teams are forced to use your platform, that's not validation—it's compliance. What you want is teams choosing your platform because it makes their lives easier.

3. Cognitive Load Reduction

This one's harder to measure but arguably more important than anything else. A platform's job is to abstract away complexity. If developers still need to understand Kubernetes, Terraform, and seventeen different AWS services to deploy a simple web app, your platform isn't doing its job.

We measure this through developer surveys with specific questions:

  1. "How many tools do you interact with for a typical deployment?" (Lower is better)
  2. "How confident are you deploying to production?" (Scale of 1-10)
  3. "How much infrastructure knowledge do you need for daily work?" (Less is better)

4. Mean Time to Recovery (MTTR)

MTTR measures how quickly teams can recover from incidents. A good platform should make recovery fast and straightforward—easy rollbacks, clear observability, and automated remediation where possible.

Track this at the platform level, not just per-team. If your platform makes rollbacks trivial, your organization's overall MTTR should decrease. If it's not improving, something's wrong with your abstractions.

5. Developer Net Promoter Score (dNPS)

Finally, ask your developers directly: "How likely are you to recommend this platform to a colleague?" This single question cuts through all the noise and tells you whether you're actually building something people value.

Run this survey quarterly. Track trends over time. And most importantly—follow up on the feedback. Nothing destroys platform trust faster than asking for input and then ignoring it.

What NOT to Measure

Just as important as knowing what to track is knowing what to ignore:

  • Number of features shipped — Activity isn't impact
  • Lines of code — More code often means more problems
  • Uptime percentages — Unless you're below 99.9%, this is noise
  • Number of support tickets — Could mean bad platform OR high adoption

Putting It Into Practice

Start small. Pick the one metric from this list that resonates most with your organization's current challenges. Build a dashboard for it. Review it weekly with your team. Make decisions based on what it tells you.

Once you've got that working, add another metric. Then another. Within six months, you'll have a clear picture of your platform's actual impact—and the data to prove it to anyone who asks.

The platforms that win aren't the ones with the most features. They're the ones that can demonstrate, with real numbers, that they make developers' lives better.
